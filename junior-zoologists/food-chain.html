Food Chain

Welcome

All living things need food to live and grow. A food chain shows who eats whom in nature. It also shows how energy moves from one living thing to another.

A food chain usually starts with a green plant, because plants make their own food using sunlight.

Parts of a Food Chain

Producer (Plants)

Producers are green plants. They make their own food using sunlight, water, and air.

Examples

  • Grass
  • Leaves
  • Trees
  • Water plants

Primary Consumer (Plant Eaters)

These animals eat plants.

Examples

  • Rabbit
  • Deer
  • Cow
  • Grasshopper
  • Zebra

Secondary Consumer

These animals eat plant-eating animals.

Examples

  • Frog
  • Snake
  • Fox
  • Small birds

Top Consumer

These are often the top predators in a food chain.

Examples

  • Lion
  • Eagle
  • Tiger
  • Crocodile
  • Shark

Example Food Chains

Each animal gets energy by eating the one before it.

Grass →Deer →Lion

  • The deer eats grass.
  • The lion eats the deer.

Leaves →Caterpillar →Bird →Eagle

  • The caterpillar eats leaves.
  • The bird eats the caterpillar.
  • The eagle eats the bird.

Grass →Grasshopper →Frog →Snake →Eagle

  • The grasshopper eats grass.
  • The frog eats the grasshopper.
  • The snake eats the frog.
  • The eagle eats the snake.

Water Plants →Small Fish →Shark

  • The small fish eats water plants.
  • The shark eats the small fish.

Why Are Food Chains Important?

Food chains help us understand:

  • How animals get food.
  • How nature stays balanced.
  • Why every plant and animal is important.

If one part of the food chain disappears, other animals may not get enough food.
